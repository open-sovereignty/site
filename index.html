What is open digital sovereignty?

Open digital sovereignty is the ability to control, verify, and evolve digital capabilities in line with your legal, operational, and strategic interests. It spans how data is held, how technology is chosen and operated, how compliance is demonstrated, and how dependencies on vendors and foreign jurisdictions are managed. Maturity is assessed across seven interrelated domains used in digital sovereignty workshops and complemented by frameworks such as the EU Cloud Sovereignty Framework.

Key pillars of digital sovereignty

Seven domains that together describe a practical sovereignty posture for cloud and digital services.

Data Sovereignty

Where data lives, how it is protected, and who controls access.

  • Data residency and location control
  • Classification, inventory, and privacy compliance
  • Cryptographic key management
  • Third-party data access policies

Technical Sovereignty

Ownership of the stack and freedom from lock-in.

  • Technology stack ownership and control
  • Vendor lock-in risk mitigation
  • Interoperability and portability strategy
  • Code and intellectual property control

Operational Sovereignty

Running and evolving services without critical foreign dependency.

  • Operational process documentation
  • Internal skills and competency development
  • Disaster recovery and business continuity
  • Operational autonomy in critical functions

Assurance Sovereignty

Evidence that controls work and compliance can be verified.

  • Audit and inspection rights
  • Sovereign security monitoring
  • Compliance framework verification
  • Independent security testing

Open Source

Transparency and influence through open ecosystems.

  • Open source strategy and policy
  • Community participation and influence
  • License compliance management
  • Internal open source expertise

Executive Oversight

Governance, budget, and accountability at leadership level.

  • Board and C-suite awareness
  • Dedicated sovereignty governance
  • Budget allocation for sovereignty
  • Strategic sovereignty planning

Managed Services

Vendor relationships with contractual control and credible exit paths.

  • Vendor selection criteria with sovereignty focus
  • Contractual sovereignty controls
  • Performance and compliance monitoring
  • Transition and exit planning
